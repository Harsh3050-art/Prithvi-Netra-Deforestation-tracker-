🌿 “When the Axe Falls, the Earth Cries”

Once dense and green, Uttarakhand’s forests are now falling silent. Every tree cut for timber, roads, or industry pushes our ecosystem closer to imbalance. The lush canopies that once protected our rivers and wildlife are vanishing — and with them, the balance of nature itself.

Problems Due to Deforestation

Deforestation has led to soil erosion, loss of biodiversity, and disruption of local climate.

Case Study

Detailed analysis of deforestation in specific regions of Uttarakhand, featuring real images of large-scale forest loss in Dehradun and other affected areas.

🏞 Overview

Between 2001 and 2024, Uttarakhand has experienced significant deforestation, especially in districts like Dehradun, Nainital, and Udham Singh Nagar. Tree cover loss has resulted in reduced forest density, biodiversity decline, and increased carbon emissions — directly impacting the fragile Himalayan ecosystem.

📍 Dehradun District

Dehradun lost about 684 hectares of tree cover from 2001–2024, a 0.44% decrease compared to the year 2000. This loss led to approximately 443 kilotons of CO₂ emissions. Nearly 80% of the loss occurred in natural forests, not plantations. In 2024 alone, around 41 hectares were lost, releasing 12.4 kt CO₂ into the atmosphere. Fires caused 52 ha of loss, while logging and land-use change caused about 632 ha.

🌲 Statewide Trends (Uttarakhand)

Across all 13 districts, forest cover has been steadily declining. Between 2001 and 2020, total forest loss was approximately 21,05,71,646 m² (2105.7 ha), while forest gain was 6,00,79,072 m² (600.8 ha). Districts like Udham Singh Nagar, Nainital, and Champawat together accounted for over 70% of the total loss. Urbanization, tourism, and industrial expansion are key contributors.

🔥 Major Causes

  • Land-Use Change – Forests cleared for agriculture, housing, and roads.
  • Logging & Timber Extraction – Both legal and illegal cutting.
  • Forest Fires – Rising due to climate change and human activities.
  • Encroachment & Development – Expanding settlements, hydro-projects, and roads.

🌱 Impact & Implications

Rising CO₂ emissions worsen climate imbalance. Soil erosion and water scarcity are increasing in hilly regions. Wildlife habitats are shrinking, leading to frequent man-animal conflicts. While reforestation efforts exist, they are not enough to offset the ongoing forest degradation.

🌍 Conclusion

The data shows that Uttarakhand’s forests are under threat, especially in Dehradun and the Terai belt. To reverse this trend, there’s an urgent need for sustainable forest management, afforestation programs, and community-based conservation efforts. Without collective action, the state’s rich green cover could face irreversible damage.

🌳 SDG 15 – Life on Land

Goal: Protect, restore, and promote sustainable use of terrestrial ecosystems, sustainably manage forests, combat desertification, halt and reverse land degradation, and halt biodiversity loss.

🌲 Deforestation & SDG 15

Aspect Description
Target 15.1 Ensure conservation, restoration, and sustainable use of terrestrial and inland freshwater ecosystems, particularly forests.
Target 15.2 Promote sustainable management of all types of forests, halt deforestation, restore degraded forests, and substantially increase afforestation and reforestation globally by 2030.
Target 15.5 Take urgent and significant action to reduce the degradation of natural habitats and halt the loss of biodiversity.
Indicator 15.2.1 Progress towards sustainable forest management (including forest area, biomass, certification, etc.).

Measures to Be Taken

1- Every infrastructure project (road widening, expressways, dams, water treatment plants) should undergo environmental impact assessment (EIA), public hearings, alternate routes or methods should be explored to minimise tree felling.
2- Before widening roads etc, look for alternatives that avoid large‐scale felling; align roads so that they cut fewer trees or adjust designs (e.g. avoid straight widening through old tree belts). Use tunnels or viaducts where feasible.
3- Use satellite imagery, NDVI indices etc to track loss of forest, so that deforestation is visible and actionable.
4- Map all existing trees, especially mature trees, so there’s better accountability before felling.
